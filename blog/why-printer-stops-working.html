Why Your Printer Stops Working (And What to Try First)

Printers have a reputation for being temperamental. They've earned it. But most problems fall into a few predictable categories.

We probably get more calls about printers than any other single piece of office equipment. "It was working yesterday" is the most common opening line. The good news is that most printer problems have straightforward causes — and a lot of them you can fix yourself before calling anyone.

The Most Common Causes

1. The Printer Lost Its Network Connection

This is the number one cause of "the printer just stopped working" calls for networked printers. The printer got a new IP address (usually after a router reboot or power outage), and now your computer is trying to talk to the old address.

What to try: Turn the printer off, wait 30 seconds, turn it back on. Check the printer's display or print a configuration page to see its current IP address. If it changed, you may need to update the printer settings on your computer or re-add the printer.

For offices with multiple computers sharing a printer, this is why we recommend setting a static IP address for printers — so they don't wander off every time the network hiccups.

2. A Print Job Got Stuck in the Queue

One bad print job can clog up the whole queue. Everything behind it just sits there waiting, and nothing prints.

What to try: Open your print queue (search "printers" in Windows, click on your printer, then "Open queue"). If you see jobs stuck there, cancel them all. Sometimes you need to restart the Print Spooler service — search "services" in Windows, find "Print Spooler," right-click, and restart it.

3. The Printer Is Set to "Offline"

Windows sometimes decides your printer is offline even when it's sitting right there, powered on and ready. This can happen after Windows updates, sleep mode, or seemingly at random.

What to try: Go to Settings > Devices > Printers & scanners, click on your printer, and look for an option to "Use Printer Online" or uncheck "Use Printer Offline." If your printer shows up multiple times in the list, you may have duplicate entries — try removing the extras.

4. Driver Problems

Windows updates occasionally break printer drivers. The printer worked fine, then an update rolled through, and now it doesn't.

What to try: Go to the printer manufacturer's website and download the latest driver for your specific model. Uninstall the old printer from your system, then install the fresh driver. This fixes a surprising number of "it just stopped working" problems.

5. The Printer Actually Has a Hardware Problem

Paper jams, empty ink or toner, worn-out rollers, or genuine mechanical failures do happen — but they're less common than the connection and software issues above.

What to try: Check for error lights or messages on the printer's display. Open it up and look for jammed paper (check all the trays and the back panel). Make sure ink or toner cartridges are properly seated.

The Quick Restart Sequence

Before doing anything else, try this sequence — it clears most transient problems:

  1. Cancel all pending print jobs on your computer
  2. Turn off the printer
  3. Wait 60 seconds (not 10 — actually wait)
  4. Turn the printer back on
  5. Wait for it to fully initialize (lights stop blinking, display shows "Ready")
  6. Try printing a test page

This solves the problem about half the time with zero troubleshooting required.

Network Printer Specifically Unreachable?

If only some computers can print and others can't, the problem is usually on the computer side, not the printer side. Check that the non-working computers are on the same network (not accidentally on a guest network), and verify they're pointed at the correct printer IP address.

If nobody can print, the printer itself probably lost its network connection — check the Ethernet cable or Wi-Fi settings on the printer.

The "Printers" Folder Lie If you look in your Windows Printers folder and see five copies of the same printer with names like "HP LaserJet (Copy 1)" and "HP LaserJet (Copy 3)" — that's a sign things have gotten messy. Each failed connection attempt often creates a new entry. Delete all of them and add the printer fresh.

When to Call

If you've tried the basics and the printer still won't cooperate, it's worth getting help. Printer troubleshooting can turn into a time sink fast, especially with older machines or complex network setups. We'd rather spend 20 minutes fixing it properly than have you spend two hours getting increasingly frustrated.

Also call if you're seeing hardware symptoms: grinding noises, paper consistently jamming in the same spot, print quality that's suddenly degraded, or error codes you can't clear. Those usually mean something mechanical needs attention.
